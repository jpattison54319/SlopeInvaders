# Source Notes

These notes summarize the source material that should guide design decisions.

## Educational game design must balance learning and game aspects

Educational games need to be both fun and educational. The learning aspect includes pedagogy, learning objectives, feedback, and how content is presented. The game aspect includes rules, interactions, world, story, aesthetics, and technology.

Design implication for Slope Invaders:

- Do not add arcade mechanics unless they support graphing behavior.
- Do not make the game a worksheet with decorations.

Source:

- Kalmpourtzis, *Educational Game Design Fundamentals*, Chapter 5, “Where to Start?”

## Games as problem-solving systems

Games can present problems in playful contexts. Polya’s problem-solving cycle is useful: understand the problem, devise a plan, carry out the plan, and look back.

Design implication for Slope Invaders:

- Each level should require students to understand coordinates/constraints, choose an equation, fire, receive feedback, and revise.

Source:

- Kalmpourtzis, *Educational Game Design Fundamentals*, Chapter 3, “Pedagogy and Games.”

## Instructions and interface clarity

Games can fail when they provide too little guidance, too much information, or unclear instructions. Instructions include rules, hints, feedback, and learning content.

Design implication for Slope Invaders:

- Introduce one mechanic at a time.
- Keep feedback short and close to the graph.
- Avoid dumping all rules at the beginning.

Source:

- Kalmpourtzis, *Educational Game Design Fundamentals*, Chapter 11, “Game Interfaces.”

## Multimedia learning and cognitive load

Game-based learning environments can overwhelm learners if visuals, controls, and curriculum demands compete for attention. Design should reduce extraneous load and support the cognitive effort related to the learning goal.

Design implication for Slope Invaders:

- Keep the graph readable.
- Use visual signals only for relevant math information.
- Avoid flashy effects during equation reasoning.

Sources:

- Nelson & Kim, “Multimedia Design Principles in Game-Based Learning.”
- Mayer, “Multimedia Learning.”

## Self-regulated learning

Game-based learning should support planning, monitoring, regulating, and reflection. Reflection is valuable, but should be embedded without breaking flow.

Design implication for Slope Invaders:

- Use immediate feedback after shots.
- Use micro-reflections after selected levels.
- Use full Mission Debrief after each zone.

Sources:

- Taub et al., “Self-Regulation and Reflection during Game-Based Learning.”
- Zheng et al., “Interweaving of self-regulated learning and game-based learning.”

## Adaptivity and personalization

Customization allows learners to change preferences. Adaptivity changes features/content based on diagnosed learner variables. Adaptability gives learners options and supports self-regulated learning. Personalization can include all of these.

Design implication for Slope Invaders:

- Cosmetic customization should be separate from learning adaptivity.
- Adaptive supports should be based on performance data.
- Student support choices should be offered when possible.

Sources:

- Plass & Pawar, “Adaptivity and Personalization in Game-Based Learning.”
- Liu et al., “Integrating adaptivity in educational games.”

## Competition

Competition in digital game-based learning can support learning outcomes, including in math contexts, but it can also distract or demotivate depending on setting and learner confidence.

Design implication for Slope Invaders:

- Multiplayer should be optional and short.
- Prefer team/class competition and improvement metrics.
- Avoid making the main learning path dependent on defeating peers.

Source:

- Chen, Shih, & Law, “The effects of competition in digital game-based learning: a meta-analysis.”
